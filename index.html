How a Synesthete Experiences Sound and Color

Imagine you're listening to music, but at the same time, your brain is automatically drawing pictures or colors for you. That's a bit like what synesthesia is.

Here's what makes it different from just thinking about colors:

It's Involuntary: A synesthete doesn't choose to see a color when they hear a note. It just happens, automatically, without any effort. It’s like how you can't help but see the color red when you look at a red apple.

It's Consistent: The colors never change. If the musical note C appears as a fiery red today, it will be the same fiery red tomorrow, next month, and ten years from now. This makes the experience reliable and stable for the person.

It's a "Second" Sense: The person still hears the sound with their ears, but they also get an additional, internal experience in their mind's eye. It’s not a hallucination, but a very real and distinct perception.

In our project, when you drag a note like E (yellow), the canvas immediately turns yellow. This is a direct model of how a synesthete's brain might work. It's a simple, consistent, and automatic connection that shows you the beauty of how their senses are cross-wired.
